The relationship between Spokane's public culture and its hidden financial dependencies reveals a complex performance of American identity. Civic celebrations, frontier iconography, and booster rhetoric all worked to establish the city as authentically Western and thoroughly American, even as Dutch bankers held the mortgage on much of its downtown.^5

The irony was that this foreign capital enabled the very development that allowed Spokane to present itself as a modern American city. Without Dutch investment, the rapid reconstruction after the fire would have been impossible. The city's American identity was thus built quite literally on a European financial foundation.^2

By the early twentieth century, this contradiction had become unsustainable. Growing nationalist sentiment, combined with economic changes in the Netherlands, led to a gradual divestment. The 1920s saw most Dutch holdings liquidated, allowing Spokane finally to match its public image with its financial reality.^4
