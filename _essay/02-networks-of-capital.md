The transformation of Spokane from frontier outpost to modern city was fundamentally shaped by transnational capital flows that originated thousands of miles away in Amsterdam's financial district. The Great Fire of 1889, while devastating to local business owners, represented an unprecedented opportunity for European investors seeking high returns in the American West.^2

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_002 action: end %}

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_004 action: start zoom: 12 coordinates: 500,400 %}

Dutch banking houses, flush with capital from South African diamond ventures, had already established a foothold in American railroad bonds. The Spokane fire allowed them to extend their influence directly into urban real estate and infrastructure development. Through the Netherlands Hypotheekbank, Dutch investors acquired controlling interests in the city's most valuable commercial properties.^3

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_004 action: end zoom: 12 coordinates: 500,400 %}

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_127 action: start zoom: 10 %}

By the mid-1890s, this financial network had created a peculiar colonial relationship. Unlike traditional European imperialism, Dutch control operated through the abstract mechanisms of mortgage debt and property ownership. The physical landscape of Spokane—its buildings, streets, and infrastructure—appeared thoroughly American, yet the underlying financial architecture was decidedly European.^4

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_127 action: end zoom: 10 %}

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_001 action: start %}

This duality generated significant anxiety among local boosters and business leaders. They celebrated the capital influx that enabled rapid reconstruction while simultaneously fearing the implications of foreign ownership. The solution was a carefully constructed public narrative that emphasized American entrepreneurship while obscuring the Dutch financial foundations upon which the new city was built.^1
