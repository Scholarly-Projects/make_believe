The visual culture of post-fire Spokane reveals the tensions between American identity and European capital. Promotional materials, postcards, and civic monuments carefully framed the city's narrative, presenting it as a triumph of frontier spirit while obscuring the foreign financial networks that made reconstruction possible.^1

Photographic evidence from the period shows a city consciously constructing its image. Architectural styles borrowed heavily from European precedents—particularly Dutch and German influences—yet public discourse emphasized American innovation and independence. This visual contradiction reflected deeper anxieties about the true nature of Spokane's development.^2

The fire itself became a founding myth, comparable to Chicago's great conflagration. Local historians emphasized the rapid rebuilding as evidence of American resilience and determination. What they consistently omitted was the crucial role of Dutch mortgage capital in financing this reconstruction.^3
