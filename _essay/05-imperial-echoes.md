The Dutch presence in Spokane represents a unique form of colonial relationship that operated through financial rather than political mechanisms. While the Netherlands never exercised formal sovereignty over American territory, its banking institutions wielded considerable economic power over the development of Western cities.^1

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_127 action: end zoom: 12 coordinates: 600,300 %}

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_003 action: start %}

This financial colonialism emerged from the particular economic geography of the late nineteenth century. Dutch capital, accumulated through diamond mining in South Africa and colonial extraction in the East Indies, sought profitable investment opportunities in growing American markets. Spokane, rebuilding after its devastating fire, offered ideal conditions.^3

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_003 action: end %}

{% trigger type: image id: make_believe_005 action: start zoom: 10 %}

The resulting relationship defied easy categorization. Dutch investors gained substantial returns and exercised significant control over urban development, yet they remained largely invisible to Spokane's residents. This invisibility was both a feature and a vulnerability of financial colonialismâ€”it could operate more smoothly than direct political control, but it also lacked the institutional permanence of formal empire.^5

Understanding this episode requires expanding our conception of colonialism beyond direct political rule. The Spokane case demonstrates how economic power, exercised through seemingly neutral financial instruments, could create relationships of dependency and control that functioned much like traditional colonial arrangements, even in the absence of formal empire.^2
